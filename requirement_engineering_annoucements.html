        • Putting it all together

          Posted on: Friday, 25 November 2016 17:30:51 o'clock GMT

          From yesterday's lecture and today’s practical on formalising use case preconditions and post conditions using an initial domain model, and using the results to drive the design of alternative sequence models, you should see the overall path we have taken on the emodule. We started with eliciting requirements from stakeholders using a range of methods including e.g ethnography and Contextual Design and representing the resulting served systems, in part, using workflowws modelled as activity diagrams; analysing the workflow models to identify potential computer based systems and their constituent use cases; representing these in requirements documents using, e.g., the Volere template; developing use case specifications from them; formalising these with the aid of identified class models; and finally creating designs, represented in sequence models,  to achieve the post conditions of use cases. Such designs could easily be given to a programmer to translate to code.

          Along the way we have looked at 21st century requirements engineering techniques like COTS, as well as at the role of the customer  representative and user stories in Agile methods.

          Next week we will look at one more way of representing requirements viz Formal Specification using the Z formal specification language. This language is built up from set theory and logic. We will discuss the key advantages and the drawbacks of using this technique. The material is available now on Blackboard for you to review.

          Regards

          Stewart

        • Next week's tutorial reading: for 21st Oct.

          Posted on: Friday, 14 October 2016 16:57:56 o'clock BST

          I would like you to read critically chapter six of the book "Contextual Design: Designing Customer-Centered Systems" by Hugh Beyer and Karen Holtzblatt. Chapter six covers the work models - flow model, sequence model, artifact model, cultural model, and physical model - used in Contextual Design. As you read try to assess critically  the extent to which they provide useful models for modelling a served system. Try to decide whether some models are more important and useful than others. Are there models that shouldn't be there? Are there models that aren't there but should be there; if so what are they. Is the notation (syntax and semantics) of each model as a clear as it should be?

          Blackboard contains the reading and also a list of questions to ponder while you read. Try to write down your answers to these questions.

          The reading is in: Learning Materials  -> Week 5 -> Tutorial - > Tutorial 1: the link is called "Tutorial 5: Work Models".

          Stewart

        • Requirements engineering in the 21st century: preparing for next week 's tutorial

          Posted on: Friday, 23 October 2015 08:21:12 o'clock BST

        • Week 3: Tutorial

          Posted on: Friday, 2 October 2015 10:07:47 o'clock BST

          The Week 1 tutorial introduced Checkland's "served system" concept and his method of "organisational analysis" for conceptualising a served system. This week's tutorial (Week 2 tutorial) has explored another method of understanding the served system: ethnography. And my indicative answers to the tutorial questions are now available for you to review. In next week's lecture, I will introduce the goal-oriented approach to requirements engineering. This method conceptualises a servde system in terms of its stakeholders goals. One of the advantages of this approach is that it encourages the identification and exploration of more than one computer-based solution that will satisfy stakeholders' goals. This raises the problem of how to choose between solutions: which solution is best?

          For next Thursday's tutorial, I would like you to read critically the paper, "Exploring Alternatives During Requirements Analysis" which is in the "Week 3: Tutorial" folder in an item called "Tutorial 3: Reading".         • Welcome to the module

          Posted on: Wednesday, 26 September 2012 12:08:28 o'clock BST

          Welcome to the undergraduate Requirements Engineering module (UFCE4S-10-3).

          The module will be presented in two halves. For the first half, in the lectures I will introduce key topics in requirements engineering, like requirements engineering in the 21st century. In the tutorial sessions we will present and discuss journal papers and book chapters on the theme of the importance of defining the served system before the serving system. You will need to read and critically review each paper or chapter before attending the associated tutorial.

          In the second half of the module, Andy Gillies, will present a key requirements engineering method called CORE (COntrolled Requirements Expression).

          You will find a schedule for the module in the Schedule section. You should familiarise yourself with this now.

          There is a link to the specimen examination paper for the module in the Course Documents section, which also contains revision notes.

          Some information will only appear on Blackboard after certain dates have passed, so you should check this site regularly.

          The essential textbook for this course is:
          Requirements Engineering: Processes and Techniques by Kotonya and Sommerville, published by John Wiley.
